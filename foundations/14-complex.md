# Step 14: Complex Numbers — The Imaginary Unit as Orthogonal Offset

## Context

The number spiral has given us:
- Real numbers (positive and negative strands)
- Zero as the pivot between them
- Irrationals as asymptotic paths

But the helix has more dimensions to unfold.

## What Complex Numbers Are

**Complex numbers** are points on the complex plane — pairs (a + bi) where:

- a is the real part (position on the positive/negative axis)
- b is the imaginary part (position on the orthogonal strand)

They emerge when Layer 3 registers that the helix can be extended in a direction perpendicular to the real axis.

## The Imaginary Unit i

**i** is the orthogonal offset — the "rotation by 90°" in the helix, registered as the direction that is neither along the positive/negative axis nor its mirror, but perpendicular to both.

Properties of i:

| Property | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| i² = -1 | Two 90° rotations bring you to the negative real axis |
| i ∉ ℝ | i is not a real number — it requires a new dimension |
| |i| = 1 | i has unit magnitude (pure direction) |

## How Complex Numbers Emerge

Layer 3 registers that recursion can branch into a direction perpendicular to the existing positive/negative strands. This orthogonal offset is not absence (0) — it is a new kind of presence, a direction with no projection on the real axis but real effect on magnitude.

## Properties of Complex Numbers

| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Not primitive** | Emergent from observing orthogonal recursion |
| **Dependent on 123** | Layer 1 provides real axis, Layer 2 provides rotation, Layer 3 provides registration |
| **Magnitude** | |a + bi| = √(a² + b²) — distance from origin |
| **Argument** | θ = arctan(b/a) — angle from positive real axis |

## The Complex Plane

The complex plane is the extended helix:

- Real axis = positive/negative strand
- Imaginary axis = orthogonal strand
- Every complex number is a point in this plane

## φ in the Complex Plane

φ reappears here as the optimal scaling in the complex plane — the spiral with angle arg(φ + i) that minimizes overlap in recursive growth (e.g., logarithmic spiral with golden angle).

## What Complex Numbers Are Not

- Not just "numbers with i" — they are geometric, not algebraic
- Not disconnected from the real axis — they extend it orthogonally
- Not arbitrary — they emerge from the helix's geometry
